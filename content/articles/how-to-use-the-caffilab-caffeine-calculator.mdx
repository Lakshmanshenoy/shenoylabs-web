## What is CaffiLab?

CaffiLab is a scientific caffeine estimation tool that calculates how much caffeine is in your coffee based on your exact brew parameters — not generic averages from a database. It uses a model grounded in extraction science:

**C = dose × bean caffeine range midpoint × recovery**

Instead of telling you "a cup of coffee has 95 mg," CaffiLab accounts for your specific coffee dose, bean species, brew method, grind size, water temperature, brew time, and more.

## Why different methods extract differently

CaffiLab doesn't use one universal formula. It assigns each brew method to a **physics class** and uses a sub-model appropriate for that class:

- **Pressure methods** (espresso): pressure differential drives fast extraction through a compacted bed. Grind is the dominant variable; ratio has a muted effect because wider pulls mainly dilute concentration rather than increase recovery percentage.
- **Percolation methods** (pour over, Chemex, drip machine, siphon, percolator): water passes through the bed under gravity, vacuum drawdown, or recirculation. Ratio matters more than for pressure — more water through the same bed genuinely extracts a higher percentage of caffeine.
- **Hot immersion methods** (French press, Turkish, immersion dripper): coffee steeps until separation. Time follows an exponential saturation curve, not a linear ramp. Turkish coffee is classified as immersion because it is a full-immersion method with no filtration — its ultra-fine grind and near-boiling temperature are already encoded in its high base recovery.
- **Cold brew**: extraction rate scales with temperature following the Arrhenius relationship. At fridge temperature (4 °C) the effective time constant is about 11 hours — meaning a 12-hour fridge brew only reaches about 65 % of the 0.88 equilibrium ceiling. At room temperature (22 °C) the time constant drops to ~7 hours and the same 12-hour steep reaches ~85 % of ceiling.
- **Hybrid methods** (AeroPress, Moka pot, Indian filter): each combines two extraction mechanisms. AeroPress uses an immersion steep with a manual press; Moka pot uses steam pressure through a compacted basket; Indian filter uses gravity plus mild steam assistance with long contact time. All three carry a +5 % base uncertainty penalty reflecting their higher recipe variability. **AeroPress (v3.6):** an **Inverted** toggle is available — selecting it adds a +0.04 recovery adjustment, reflecting the elimination of drip-through during the steep phase in the inverted setup.
- **Cold drip**: like cold brew but single-pass percolation — equilibrium is never reached, so the ceiling is lower.

## Getting started: the basic inputs

When you open CaffiLab, you'll see the core inputs that drive the estimate:

### 1. Brew method

Select your brewing method. This sets sensible defaults for grind size, brew time, temperature, and extraction expectations, and also selects the appropriate physics class for the recovery model.

### 2. Bean species

Choose between Arabica, Robusta, Blend, or "Not sure." Robusta usually carries a meaningfully higher caffeine range than Arabica, so this choice has a large impact on the estimate.

If you select "Not sure," you can also provide a **Package clue** — what the bag or label says about the coffee — to help CaffiLab infer a likely arabica/robusta blend profile:

- **Specialty single origin** — infers ~90 % Arabica, 10 % Robusta.
- **Espresso blend** — infers 75 % Arabica, 25 % Robusta.
- **South Indian filter blend** — infers 50 % Arabica, 50 % Robusta.
- **Commercial / instant** — infers ~25 % Arabica, 75 % Robusta.
- **None** (default) — no label clue; CaffiLab falls back to price-based inference if price is provided, otherwise uses the default 70/30 blend.

Providing a package clue reduces brewing uncertainty by 5 percentage points — the single largest uncertainty reduction available for an unknown-species estimate.

### 3. Bean detail

Use the optional **Bean detail** control if you know more about the bean than just the species:

- **Generic range** keeps the standard species range.
- **High-altitude** biases toward the lower-caffeine side of that range.
- **Low-altitude** biases toward the higher-caffeine side.
- **Custom caffeine %** lets you enter a measured or published dry-weight caffeine percentage.

You can also select the **Processing method** for the beans:

- **Washed** — clean cellular structure, slight extraction advantage (+0.010 for hot methods).
- **Honey** — partial mucilage, neutral effect.
- **Natural** — dried fruit pulp residue creates a mild diffusion barrier (−0.012 for hot methods).
- **Unknown** (default) — no adjustment.

For cold brew, the processing method effect is damped by 70 % because low temperature already suppresses the mucilage-barrier mechanism.

### 4. Coffee dose

Enter the weight of dry coffee grounds you're using. More coffee means more available caffeine — this is a direct multiplier in the formula.

### 5. Brew water

The volume of water you're brewing with. This determines your coffee-to-water ratio, which affects extraction efficiency. CaffiLab shows your current ratio and the method's target ratio.

### 6. Beverage volume

The final volume of liquid in your cup, including any dilution. This affects concentration (mg per 100 ml) but not total caffeine.

### 7. Brew time

How long water is in contact with the coffee grounds. The effect depends heavily on the physics class:

- For **hot immersion** (French press, Turkish), extraction saturates after about 8 minutes. Going to 20 minutes adds very little.
- For **cold brew**, time matters enormously — the difference between 6 h and 18 h at fridge temperature is much larger than the equivalent time range for a hot method.
- For **pressure methods** (espresso), brew time affects but doesn't dominate — grind size and pressure are the primary controls.

## Understanding the results

CaffiLab shows four key outputs:

- **Estimated caffeine (mg):** The headline number — total milligrams of caffeine in your cup.
- **Confidence label:** Very High, High, Medium, or Low — based on both brewing uncertainty and the bean-variability floor.
- **Precision bar:** A visual gauge showing how tight the estimate is.
- **Practical range:** The lower and upper bounds after the final uncertainty cap is applied.

You will also see the uncertainty sources broken out:

- **Bean variability:** the uncertainty caused by the bean's caffeine range itself.
- **Brewing uncertainty:** the uncertainty caused by missing or approximate brew inputs.
- **Applied cap:** the larger of those two values. Confidence cannot outrun bean variability.

## Fine-tuning with advanced inputs

Click **Advanced inputs** to reveal additional parameters:

- **Grind size:** Finer grinds expose more surface area, increasing extraction.
- **Grinder type (v3.5, updated v3.6):** Blade grinders produce an uneven bimodal particle distribution — coarse boulders and fine powder. The penalty now varies by brewing method: **−0.025** for percolation/pressure methods (boulders create flow channels), **−0.018** for hybrid methods (AeroPress, Moka), and **−0.010** for immersion methods (fines partially compensate for boulders). For cold methods this effect is damped by 70 %. Select **Burr** for zero penalty or **Unknown** for a conservative −0.008.
- **Agitation / stirring:** For methods that support it (pour over, Chemex, French press, cold brew, AeroPress, siphon, Turkish, immersion), declare how vigorously you stir or agitate during brewing. **None** is the conservative default baseline. **Gentle** (+0.008) helps even wetting. **Moderate** (+0.018) promotes consistent extraction across the bed. **High** (+0.026) maximises surface contact. Declaring any non-None level reduces brewing uncertainty by 2 percentage points. Not applicable to espresso, Moka pot, drip machine, Indian filter, cold drip, or percolator — these methods are automated or enclosed and do not expose an agitation input.
- **Roast level:** From v3.4, CaffiLab uses a non-linear roast mass-balance curve: light (×0.99), medium (×0.97), dark (×0.92), and very dark — French or Italian roast — (×0.88). Heavy roasting degrades significantly more caffeine than the model previously assumed. Select **Very Dark** for heavily charred or French / Italian roasts.
- **Temperature:** Higher temperatures increase extraction rate significantly. For cold brew, entering your actual brew temperature (fridge vs room temp) changes the model's time constant.
- **Bean detail / custom caffeine %:** Refines the bean caffeine range if you know more than the species label.
- **Extraction yield:** If you measure TDS (total dissolved solids), entering yield is one of the strongest ways to calibrate the estimate.
- **Pressure:** Only for espresso — 9 bar is the reference point.
- **Water hardness (ppm):** Enter the hardness of your brewing water in parts per million (ppm CaCO₃). Balanced water (50–150 ppm) is closest to specialty brewing standards and earns a small +0.005 extraction bonus. Very soft water (&lt;50 ppm) and very hard water (&gt;250 ppm) each apply a small −0.005 penalty. Leave blank for no adjustment.
- **Water pH:** Enter your water pH if known. Near-neutral water (6.0–7.5) earns a +0.002 bonus. Acidic (&lt;6.0) or alkaline (&gt;7.5) water applies a small penalty. Leave blank for no adjustment. **Note (v3.6):** if both hardness > 250 ppm and pH > 7.5, an additional −0.007 high-buffer interaction penalty applies, capturing the synergistic carbonate resistance to extraction.
- **Bean freshness:** Four options shift the extraction recovery estimate:
  - **Fresh** (roasted within ~2 weeks): −0.008 — CO₂ off-gassing from recently roasted beans can cause uneven wetting and channeling, counterintuitively reducing extraction efficiency.
  - **Rested** (2–6 weeks off roast): no adjustment — the optimal window when CO₂ has dissipated but volatiles remain.
  - **Stale** (6+ weeks): −0.010 — degraded aromatics reduce diffusion efficiency (caffeine content itself is thermally stable).
  - **Unknown** (default): no adjustment.
  Declaring any known freshness (other than Unknown) reduces brewing uncertainty by 3 percentage points.
- **Filter type:** Four options affect extraction efficiency based on what the brew medium allows through:
  - **Paper** (−0.004): filters out oils and fine particles, slightly lowering extraction recovery.
  - **Metal** (+0.006): allows oils and more suspended solids, marginally boosting recovery.
  - **Cloth** (no adjustment): neutral — used by default for siphon, cold brew, and cold drip.
  - **None** (+0.006): no filtration medium — Turkish coffee brews directly in the cup; same effect as metal.
  CaffiLab sets the default filter type automatically based on your brew method selection.
- **Chicory:** For Indian filter coffee — chicory adds body but no caffeine.

Each brewing input you declare can reduce brewing uncertainty. But if the bean itself still has a wide caffeine range, CaffiLab keeps that uncertainty visible instead of hiding it.

**Note on cold brew temperature:** If you don't enter a brew temperature, CaffiLab assumes room temperature (22 °C). If you brew in the fridge (4 °C), entering that temperature significantly changes the estimate — fridge cold brew needs ~40 % more time to reach the same extraction as room-temperature cold brew.

## Going deeper with Expert inputs

Click **Expert inputs** for three additional bean-quality parameters that further narrow the caffeine fraction range:

- **Arabica grade:** Splits the arabica range into Specialty (1.2–1.6 % dry weight) and Commercial (1.0–1.2 %). Most specialty-roaster bags are Specialty grade.
- **Growing elevation:** Four bands describe the farm altitude and shift the estimated caffeine fraction range:
  - **Unknown** (default): no shift applied.
  - **Low** (below ~800 m): shifts the fraction range downward by 15 % of its width — lower-altitude farms typically sit in the lower part of the species range.
  - **Mid** (800–1500 m): reference band, no shift (same as Unknown in terms of fraction, but declaring it removes 2 pp of brewing uncertainty).
  - **High** (above ~1500 m): shifts the fraction range upward by 15 % of its width. From v3.2, also applies a small extraction recovery correction (×0.98) when brew temperature is below 95 °C — denser cell walls at high altitude slightly impede solvent penetration.
  When High elevation is set alongside **Origin / Region**, the ×0.95 interaction correction described in that section is applied automatically to avoid double-counting the terroir signal.
- **Extraction quality (espresso only):** Accounts for channeling and puck preparation. A poorly tamped puck can meaningfully reduce caffeine extraction compared to a well-prepared shot.
- **Cultivar:** Named arabica cultivar shifts the estimated bean caffeine fraction based on published measurements:
  - **Geisha** (~0.9–1.1 % dry weight) — distinctly lower than typical arabica.
  - **SL28** (~1.3–1.7 %) — Kenyan variety with elevated caffeine.
  - **Caturra** (~1.0–1.3 %) — Bourbon-derived, mid-range arabica.
  - **Catimor** (~1.5–2.0 %) — Robusta hybrid, sits notably higher.
  - **Unknown** is the default if you are not sure which variety you have.
- **Origin / Region:** Select the broad growing region where the beans were produced. CaffiLab applies a regional multiplier to the caffeine fraction based on known terroir and varietal patterns:
  - **India / Southeast Asia** applies a +7.5 % factor on the midpoint estimate (range: ×1.05–1.10). Coffees from India, Vietnam, and Indonesia tend toward higher-caffeine varieties and growing conditions.
  - **Africa** and **Latin America** are the reference baseline (factor: 1.00). Ethiopian, Kenyan, Colombian, and Brazilian lots are well-represented in the calibration data.
  - **Not sure (default)** leaves the factor at 1.00. Use this if you do not know the origin.
  - Providing any known region also tightens the confidence interval slightly by reducing the base brewing uncertainty by 1 percentage point.
  - **Elevation × region interaction (v3.1):** If you also set **Growing Elevation**, the region factor is multiplied by ×0.95 automatically. This prevents double-counting: elevation already partially captures the terroir signal that the region factor represents. The applied factor is shown in the *Model factors* section of the printed report.
- **Growing Elevation:** High-altitude beans develop more slowly and may accumulate slightly different caffeine concentrations. When this is set alongside Origin / Region, the ×0.95 interaction correction described above is applied. Additionally, from v3.2, selecting **High** elevation applies a small extraction recovery correction (×0.98): denser bean cell walls common at high altitude slightly impede solvent penetration during brewing. From v3.6 this correction only applies when the brew temperature is below 95 °C — at or above boiling, thermal energy overcomes the cell-wall density effect. This is independent of the caffeine fraction adjustment — it acts on the extraction efficiency, not the bean content.

These Expert inputs do not change the brewing recovery model — they refine the starting caffeine fraction in the bean before any extraction calculation.

**v3.6 monitoring & safety notes:**

- **ΔMinor monitoring:** If the `ΔMinor` term repeatedly hits the +0.02 clamp, CaffiLab surfaces a "Review recommended" flag in the *Model factors* panel and suggests collecting calibration samples where practical.
- **Global sensitivity cap:** When declared secondary inputs collectively exceed 60% total influence, CaffiLab proportionally scales secondary deltas and shows a "Sensitivity cap applied" badge so the headline estimate remains interpretable.
- **β stability guard:** Small-sample β updates are inhibited when the proposed change exceeds 0.02 and fewer than 10 samples are present; the app will prompt for more calibration data.
## Confidence bounds and what they mean

CaffiLab reports a **range** (e.g., 151–214 mg) alongside the central estimate. These bounds are computed from two independent uncertainty sources combined in quadrature — see [how the formula works](/articles/how-does-caffilab-formula-work) for the full derivation.

From v3.1, the bounds are **asymmetric**: the upper bound is 2 percentage points wider than the lower. Under-extraction is more constrained in practice than over-extraction (especially for espresso and pressure methods), so the upside risk is deliberately wider.

## Calibration status

CaffiLab's output passes through two calibration layers before display:

- **Global α** — a single scaling factor applied to the final estimate. Currently 1.0 (no adjustment). Computed using `median(measured / predicted)` across all samples — the median is used rather than the mean so that a single miscalibrated measurement cannot distort the factor.
- **Per-method β** — a separate factor for each extraction physics class. The `cold_immersion` class (cold brew) now has **β = 1.10**, anchored from 10 direct-HPLC samples from Fuller &amp; Rao (2017, *Scientific Reports*, [10.1038/s41598-017-18247-4](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-017-18247-4)) and Stanek et al. (2021, *Scientific Reports*, [10.1038/s41598-021-01001-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-01001-2)). All other classes remain at 1.0 pending sufficient data. Requires at least 5 samples per class before it is applied; clamped to [0.9, 1.1] to prevent overfitting.

Both values are shown in the *Model factors* section of the printed PDF report. A value of 1.000 means the architecture is in place but the model has not yet been empirically anchored for that class.

**Dataset schema.** Each calibration sample records method, dose, volume, grind, brew time, temperature, roast, bean type, the model's prediction, and the measured caffeine value. The first available calibration pool is the Cotter / Ristenpart UC Davis drip-coffee dataset (3186 samples; Dryad DOI: [10.25338/B8993H](https://doi.org/10.25338/B8993H)), which covers the percolation physics class at three temperatures (87 / 90 / 93 °C) and three extraction levels.

## Tips for accurate estimates

1. **Weigh your coffee.** Scoops vary; a kitchen scale makes the dose precise.
2. **Measure your water.** Know how much water goes in and how much beverage comes out.
3. **Time your brew.** Especially for immersion methods like French press and cold brew.
4. **For cold brew, enter the actual brew temperature.** The difference between 4 °C and 22 °C substantially changes the extraction rate calculation.
5. **Start with defaults.** CaffiLab auto-fills sensible values. Adjust only what you know.
6. **Use Bean detail only when you truly know it.** Generic is the right choice unless you have a good reason to narrow the range.
7. **Check the explanation panel.** As you interact with each field, CaffiLab explains what it does and how it affects the estimate.

## Interactive features

CaffiLab includes several interactive panels designed to help you understand why the number changes as you adjust inputs.

### Extraction profile

Every brew method displays an **Extraction profile** chart immediately below the main stats. The curve shows the shape of caffeine extraction over time for that method's physics family:

- **Pressure-driven** (espresso): a sharp spike then a plateau — fast extraction at high pressure.
- **Percolation** (pour over, Chemex, drip): a smooth rise to a stable plateau as water passes through the bed.
- **Immersion** (French press, Turkish): an S-curve rising toward saturation — time matters but diminishing returns set in.
- **Cold immersion** (cold brew): a very gradual climb over many hours reflecting Arrhenius-limited diffusion.
- **Cold drip**: a steady rise that never fully plateaus because single-pass contact time is limited.
- **Hybrid** (AeroPress, Moka pot, Indian filter): a combined shape reflecting the dual extraction mechanism.

The curve **draws itself in** each time you switch brew method — a smooth left-to-right animation that makes the extraction shape easy to read. A gold **"Your brew" indicator** moves along the curve as you change inputs, showing where your current extraction recovery falls on the time axis. The indicator dot has a soft glow and gentle pulse so it is easy to spot at a glance.

### Method hint

When you select a brew method, a short description appears below the selector summarising its extraction character — for example, "High pressure · Fast extraction · Incomplete recovery" for espresso or "Low temperature · Time-driven equilibrium · Slow diffusion" for cold brew.

### How was this calculated?

Below the uncertainty cards in the result panel, tap **How was this calculated?** to expand a visual breakdown of the three factors driving the headline number:

- **Dose (G):** your coffee mass in grams.
- **Bean strength (F_mid):** the effective caffeine fraction for the declared or inferred species.
- **Extraction (E):** the caffeine recovery percentage for this method and inputs.

Each factor is shown as a proportional bar so you can see at a glance whether dose, bean choice, or extraction efficiency is contributing most to the result.

### Confidence range bar

The range bar below the headline estimate is colour-coded and animated. It shows:

- The **practical range** (min – max) as a gradient band — the band itself transitions smoothly whenever the range widens or narrows.
- A **marker line** at the estimated value.
- **Colour coding** based on confidence: green for a narrow range, gold for moderate, orange for wide. The track has a subtle warm gradient so you can see at a glance where on the confidence spectrum your current inputs sit.

The bean-driven range is shown in small text below.

### What changed

Below the confidence bar, a **What changed** list appears as soon as you start adjusting inputs. Each new row slides up into view, showing which field you just changed and by how much — for example, "Dose +22 mg" or "Grind size -4 mg". The list keeps the last four changes and is sorted by absolute impact — the input that moved the estimate the most appears at the top, highlighted in gold.

The headline caffeine number **counts up or down** to the new value rather than jumping, making the effect of each input change feel immediate and tangible. A small delta badge (+18 in green or -12 in red) fades in next to the number with each change.

### Advanced science section

Below Expert inputs, an **Advanced science** section (collapsed by default) contains explanations of the three core model components for users who want to understand the mechanics:

- **Semi-mechanistic model:** how physics-informed extraction recovery and empirically calibrated secondary adjustments combine.
- **Arrhenius cold brew model:** the temperature-dependent diffusivity equation governing cold brew and cold drip extraction.
- **Uncertainty model:** how bean variability and brewing uncertainty combine into the final confidence cap.

## A note on accuracy

CaffiLab is an estimation tool, not a laboratory instrument. Actual caffeine content varies with cultivar, altitude, growing conditions, roast batch, water chemistry, and brewing geometry. The model is calibrated against published extraction research and aims to give you a useful, directionally accurate number — not a precise chemical measurement.

Known limitations: grind particle distribution beyond a blade/burr distinction is not modelled; processing residual sugar and pectin levels vary within a single processing style and are not captured; and brewing altitude is not corrected for (if you brew above 2000 m, your actual water temperature may be lower than what you enter, since boiling point decreases with altitude).

For most practical purposes — understanding whether you're having 100 mg or 300 mg, comparing brew methods, or tracking daily intake — CaffiLab gives you a much better answer than generic tables.
