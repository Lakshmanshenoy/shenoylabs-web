## The core model

CaffiLab now estimates caffeine with a range-aware multiplicative model:

- **Estimate** = **G × F_mid × E × 1000**
- **Bean lower bound** = **G × F_min × E × 1000**
- **Bean upper bound** = **G × F_max × E × 1000**

Where:

- **G** = coffee dose in grams
- **F_min / F_max / F_mid** = the bean caffeine range and its midpoint
- **E** = caffeine recovery (what fraction of the available caffeine actually ends up in the cup)

The result is multiplied by 1000 to convert from grams to milligrams.

This matters because coffee beans do not have a single fixed caffeine value. Even before brewing starts, species, growing conditions, and blend composition create a real baseline range.

These are dry-weight working ranges used by the calculator. They are intentionally narrower than the full botanical extremes reported in the literature, because CaffiLab is trying to represent realistic consumer coffee while avoiding false precision.

**Key references:**

- Farah, A. (2012). *Coffee Constituents.* In Y.-F. Chu (Ed.), Coffee: Emerging Health Effects and Disease Prevention. Wiley-Blackwell. Reports typical Arabica caffeine at 1.0–1.3% and Robusta at 1.7–4.0%.
- Ky, C.-L., et al. (2001). "Caffeine, trigonelline, chlorogenic acids and sucrose diversity in wild *Coffea arabica* L. and *C. canephora* P. accessions." *Food Chemistry*, 75(2), 223–230. Measured caffeine across wild accessions, confirming Arabica typically near 1.2%.
- Campa, C., et al. (2005). "Trigonelline and sucrose diversity in wild *Coffea* species." *Food Chemistry*, 88(1), 39–43. Extended species-level caffeine measurements.

### Blend handling

For blends, CaffiLab computes a weighted range:

**F_min,blend = (arabica% × F_min,arabica + robusta% × F_min,robusta) / 100**

**F_max,blend = (arabica% × F_max,arabica + robusta% × F_max,robusta) / 100**

**F_mid,blend = (F_min,blend + F_max,blend) / 2**

When the user doesn't know the species, CaffiLab uses a fallback hierarchy:

1. **Package clues** (e.g., "specialty single origin" → mostly Arabica; "commercial instant" → Robusta-heavy)
2. **Price inference** (higher prices correlate with Arabica-forward blends)
3. **Conservative default** (100% Arabica if nothing is known)

### Bean detail input

The advanced panel adds an optional **Bean detail** input:

- **Generic range:** Uses the full species range.
- **High-altitude:** Biases toward the lower-caffeine half of the species range.
- **Low-altitude:** Biases toward the higher-caffeine half of the species range.
- **Custom caffeine %:** Uses a user-supplied dry-weight caffeine percentage.

This lets the model acknowledge bean-level variability directly instead of pretending the species average is exact.

### Chicory adjustment

For Indian filter coffee, chicory is a common additive. Chicory contributes essentially zero caffeine (McCusker et al., 2003), so the effective caffeine fraction is reduced:

**F_effective = F × (1 − chicory% / 100)**

### Expert-mode bean fraction adjustments

The Expert inputs panel adds three further refinements to the bean caffeine fraction range (F_range). These are applied in order after the species and blend steps:

#### Arabica grade

The arabica species range (1.0–1.6 %) is split into two quality tiers:

- **Specialty** (1.2–1.6 %): washed/natural single-origins, high-quality traded lots.
- **Commercial** (1.0–1.2 %): commodity-grade arabica, typical of mainstream blends.

#### Growing elevation

Farm elevation correlates with caffeine content through UV exposure and pest-pressure stress responses (Bertrand et al., 2003):

- **High** (> 1500 m): shifts the blended range upward by ~15 % of its width.
- **Mid** (800–1500 m): no shift (reference band).
- **Low** (< 800 m): shifts the blended range downward by ~15 % of its width.

#### Cultivar

Named arabica cultivars have measurably different caffeine concentrations. CaffiLab applies an absolute shift to the blended F_range, weighted by the arabica proportion of the blend:

### Adjustment factors

CaffiLab modifies the base recovery with 12 adjustment functions, each applying a small multiplier:

1. **Roast level:** Medium roast is the reference baseline (zero adjustment). Light roasts can show slightly higher caffeine in comparable brews — Ludwig et al. (2014) found ~2–3% higher caffeine in light vs. medium filter brews — so CaffiLab applies a small positive for light. Dark roast receives a modest negative adjustment; HPLC studies (Hečimović et al., 2011; Ludwig et al., 2014) show only a ~2–3% difference vs. medium, so the penalty is kept proportionate.

2. **Grind size:** Finer grinds increase surface area and extraction. The adjustment is relative to the method's default grind — going finer than default increases recovery, coarser decreases it.

3. **Brew time:** For hot methods, caffeine extracts rapidly in the first 1–2 minutes, then plateaus (Gloess et al., 2013). CaffiLab uses a logarithmic curve. For cold methods, the model rises quickly early and flattens after ~7 hours (Fuller & Rao, 2017).

4. **Temperature:** Higher temperatures increase extraction rate. The adjustment is calibrated around the method's default temperature.

5. **Brew ratio:** The coffee-to-water ratio affects extraction yield. Ratios tighter than the method default slightly increase recovery; wider ratios decrease it.

6. **Extraction yield:** If the user provides a measured TDS/yield, this is the strongest calibrator — it directly scales recovery.

7. **Pressure (espresso only):** Around 9 bar is the reference. Deviations apply a modest adjustment.

8. **Agitation:** Stirring increases contact efficiency for immersion methods.

9. **Water minerals:** Balanced mineral water extracts more efficiently than very soft or very hard water (Hendon et al., 2014).

10. **Freshness:** Very fresh beans resist even extraction; stale beans extract less predictably.

11. **Filter type:** Paper filters produce slightly lower measured caffeine than metal or no-filter methods.

12. **Water pH:** Caffeine is a weak base with a pKa of ~10.4, meaning it is effectively neutral across the entire brewing pH range (6–8). Slightly acidic water (pH < 6.5) has a minor positive effect on caffeine solubility; alkaline water (pH > 7.8) slightly inhibits extraction. The net effect on caffeine is small compared to temperature or grind size.

### Recovery clamping

After all adjustments, recovery is clamped to the range **0.42–0.97**. This prevents physically implausible values — you can't extract negative caffeine or more than exists.

## Uncertainty model

CaffiLab now splits uncertainty into two sources:

1. **Bean variability**: an irreducible floor based on the caffeine range itself.
2. **Brewing uncertainty**: a reducible layer based on how many brewing details you provide.

### Bean variability uncertainty

Bean variability is computed as:

**bean_uncertainty = (F_max − F_min) / (2 × F_mid)**

This means confidence cannot become artificially high if the bean itself still has a meaningful caffeine spread.

### Brewing uncertainty

Brewing uncertainty still reflects what you do and do not know about the recipe. It starts broad and shrinks when you declare more useful inputs, such as:

- brew time
- extraction yield
- grind size
- pressure
- water chemistry
- freshness
- filter type
- and other brewing controls

### Applied confidence cap

The calculator then uses:

**total_uncertainty = max(bean_uncertainty, brewing_uncertainty)**

So even if the brewing inputs are excellent, confidence is capped by the bean variability floor unless you narrow that floor with bean detail or a custom caffeine percentage.

That produces two useful outputs:

- **Bean-driven range:** based only on the bean caffeine range.
- **Practical range:** based on the final applied uncertainty cap.

## Limitations

1. **Remaining variation:** Even with cultivar, grade, and elevation specified, individual lots within a cultivar still vary with processing method, harvest season, and storage conditions. The practical range output reflects this residual uncertainty.

2. **Brew geometry:** The shape and flow dynamics of a pour-over dripper or espresso basket affect extraction in ways the model doesn't capture.

3. **Roasting loss:** Roasting converts a small fraction of caffeine to methyluric acids. This is accounted for qualitatively (via the roast adjustment) but not with precise mass-balance.

4. **No lab validation:** CaffiLab's estimates are calibrated against published research, not against HPLC measurements of its own output.
