## Understanding your output range

CaffiLab always returns three values: a central estimate and a lower and upper bound.

**Why the range is not symmetric**

The upper bound is always 2 percentage points wider than the lower bound. This is intentional. Under-extraction has a natural floor — you cannot extract caffeine that was never solubilised. Over-extraction, particularly in pressure methods, can push recovery higher than the baseline model predicts through channelling effects, fine grind migration, and extended contact. Real-world measurements skew slightly high more often than they skew low.

Example at 15 % combined uncertainty:

```text
Lower bound uses: 15 %  →  estimate × 0.85
Upper bound uses: 17 %  →  estimate × 1.17
```

**Why the two uncertainty sources are combined in quadrature**

Bean variability (the caffeine range of your beans) and brewing uncertainty (how precisely your brew is modelled) are independent. Combining them by addition would overstate total uncertainty; combining them by maximum would understate it. Quadrature is the statistically correct combination for independent sources:

```text
total_uncertainty = sqrt(bean_uncertainty² + brewing_uncertainty²)
```

This means declaring more brewing inputs narrows the range, but it cannot eliminate the bean variability floor — that only narrows when species or bean detail is specified.

## The core model

CaffiLab estimates caffeine with a range-aware multiplicative model:

- **Estimate** = **G × F_mid × E × 1000**
- **Bean lower bound** = **G × F_min × E × 1000**
- **Bean upper bound** = **G × F_max × E × 1000**

Where:

- **G** = coffee dose in grams
- **F_min / F_max / F_mid** = the bean caffeine fraction and its midpoint (dry-weight %)
- **E** = caffeine recovery — the fraction of available caffeine that actually ends up in the cup
- **× 1000** converts grams to milligrams

The recovery term **E** is the most complex part. Because every brew method works differently at a physical level, CaffiLab uses **method-class-specific sub-models** rather than a single additive formula. The next section explains this architecture.

## How formulas appear in the PDF report

When you export a CaffiLab report, the formulas are shown in two layers:

1. **Primary estimation formula** (prominent):
	- Estimate = G × F_mid × E × 1000
	- Includes substitution with your live values and final mg output.
2. **Residual caffeine decay formula** (secondary):
	- C = C0 × (1/2)^(t / half_life)
	- Used to show remaining caffeine across the crash and post-crash timeline.

This presentation is intentional: users see the practical headline result first, then the authenticity math directly below it.

> **Model type:** CaffiLab uses a semi-mechanistic model — core extraction behavior is physics-informed, while secondary adjustments are empirically calibrated.

## Per-class extraction models

In every class, three secondary variables — water hardness, water pH, and bean freshness — are combined into a single bounded term (v3.5: previously `ΔWater` combined both water factors into one lookup; they are now independent; v3.5.2: high-buffer interaction term added):

```text
ΔMinor = clamp(ΔHardness + ΔpH + ΔHighBuffer + ΔFreshness, −0.02, +0.02)
```

**Water hardness breakpoints (ΔHardness):**

**High-buffer interaction (ΔHighBuffer, v3.5.2):**

When water pH > 7.5 **and** hardness > 250 ppm simultaneously, the carbonate / bicarbonate buffer system amplifies resistance to caffeine extraction beyond what the independent ΔHardness and ΔpH terms capture. Each factor alone is a first-order inhibitor; combined, they create a reinforcing buffer matrix that more aggressively resists solvation:

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Condition</th>
      <th>ΔHighBuffer</th>
      <th>Rationale</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Hardness &gt; 250 ppm <strong>and</strong> pH &gt; 7.5</td><td>−0.007</td><td>Synergistic carbonate-buffer resistance to extraction</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Otherwise</td><td>0.000</td><td>No additional interaction penalty</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

The ±0.02 bound is empirically derived from the literature on secondary extraction variables. Individually, each factor has a measured maximum effect of 0.5–1.5% on caffeine recovery (Hendon et al., 2014 for water mineralogy; Ludwig et al., 2014 for bean freshness). Combined, these secondary factors rarely exceed 2% total recovery change in controlled experiments. The clamp prevents implausible compounding when all sub-adjustments push in the same direction simultaneously — a scenario unlikely in practice but expressible in the input space.

This bound is deliberately conservative. If future calibration data shows secondary factors consistently reaching the 2% ceiling, the bound should be reviewed and the variables should be elevated to independent terms.

Filter type contributes an independent small bounded adjustment (~±0.01):

```text
ΔFilter = getFilterAdjustment(filterType)
```

Both `ΔMinor` and `ΔFilter` enter `totalDelta` separately. `ΔFilter` is independently bounded (±0.01) and applied separately from `ΔMinor`, allowing its effect to remain visible but controlled. ΔRoast remains separate because roasting has a larger and more direct effect on soluble-caffeine content.

### Grinder type penalty (v3.5, scaled by physics class in v3.5.2)

Blade grinders produce a bimodal particle distribution (coarse boulders + fine powder). The penalty depends on the brewing physics class because the bimodal distribution interacts with extraction mechanics differently:

- **Percolation / pressure**: boulders create preferential flow channels, amplifying under-extraction. Full penalty applies.
- **Immersion**: fines over-extract and partially compensate for the boulders' under-extraction. Reduced penalty.
- **Hybrid**: intermediate between immersion and percolation archetypes.
- **Cold methods**: callers additionally scale all grinder adjustments by ×0.3.

### Pressure methods (espresso)

Pressure forces water through a compacted bed in seconds. Andueza et al. (2003) showed that caffeine recovery peaks near 9 bar for espresso; over- and under-extraction both reduce efficiency.

The recovery for pressure methods is computed as:

```text
totalDelta = ΔPressure_deviation + ΔGrind + ΔTemp + ΔTime
           + ΔRatio × 0.025 + ΔYield + ΔTechnique
           + ΔRoast + ΔProcessing + ΔGrinderType + ΔFilter + ΔMinor

E = clamp(E₀ × (1 + totalDelta), 0.45, 0.88)
```

Key characteristics:
- **Ratio sensitivity is intentionally muted** (×0.025 scaling): a longer pull produces more beverage volume, not fundamentally higher caffeine recovery percentage.
- **Grind is the dominant variable** — it controls both surface area and channeling risk.
- **Pressure adjustment**: models only the signed deviation from the 9 bar reference — positive above 9 bar, negative below. E₀ already encodes 9 bar performance; no extra bonus is applied at the reference point.

---

### Percolation methods (pour over, Chemex, drip machine, siphon, percolator)

Water passes through the bed under gravity, vacuum drawdown (siphon), or repeated hot-water recirculation (percolator). Gloess et al. (2013) measured 75–90 % caffeine recovery for filter methods.

```text
totalDelta = ΔGrind + ΔTemp + ΔTime
           + ΔRatio × 0.040 + ΔAgitation + ΔYield
           + ΔRoast + ΔProcessing + ΔGrinderType + ΔFilter + ΔMinor

E = clamp(E₀ × (1 + totalDelta), 0.55, 0.97)
```

Key characteristics:
- **Ratio sensitivity is higher** than pressure methods (×0.040 scaling): more water through the same bed volume genuinely extracts a higher percentage of caffeine.
- **Siphon** (vacuum-assisted): the drawdown ensures consistent contact temperature; its `defaultRecovery` (0.83) reflects this consistency.
- **Percolator** (recirculating): near-boiling hot-water cycling pushes its `defaultRecovery` (0.90) to the top of the percolation range. Adjustments scale from this high baseline. A **+5 % uncertainty penalty** is applied to reflect high cycle-to-cycle variability (inconsistent cycling rate, temperature stability, and contact time). This reflects both thermal instability and repeated extraction cycles that can vary significantly across devices.
- **Agitation** (bloom, pour technique) modestly improves extraction uniformity.

---

### Hot immersion methods (French press, Turkish, immersion dripper)

Coffee steeps in water until mechanically separated. Extraction follows a rapid early phase then approaches a diffusion-limited equilibrium. The model uses an exponential saturation curve rather than a linear time adjustment. Turkish coffee is classified here because it is a full-immersion method (extra-fine grind, no filtration, near-boiling) — its `defaultRecovery` (0.89) reflects the combined advantage of fine grind and high temperature, not a separate physics class.

The time factor uses:

```text
tau    = 4.0 min  (time constant: reaches ~63% of equilibrium at 4 min)
eqTime = method default brew time (the reference equilibrium)

normalized = (1 - exp(-t / tau)) / (1 - exp(-eqTime / tau))
ΔTime      = clamp((normalized - 1) × 0.07, -0.12, +0.04)
```

Full recovery:

```text
totalDelta = ΔTime + ΔGrind + ΔTemp
           + ΔRatio × 0.020 + ΔAgitation + ΔYield
           + ΔRoast + ΔProcessing + ΔGrinderType + ΔFilter + ΔMinor

E = clamp(E₀ × (1 + totalDelta), 0.55, 0.95)
```

Key characteristics:
- **Ratio sensitivity is lowest** of the hot methods (×0.020): at equilibrium, the same total caffeine is in solution regardless of volume.
- **Grind has low sensitivity** past ~8 min: finer grind speeds early extraction but adds almost nothing at equilibrium.
- **Time follows a saturation curve**, not a linear ramp. Adding more time past equilibrium yields no further extraction.

### Cold immersion (cold brew)

Cold brew is the most physically distinct method. Low temperature reduces the diffusion coefficient of caffeine from the cell matrix, following the Arrhenius relationship:

```text
k_hot ∝ exp(-Ea / R·T_hot)
k_cold ∝ exp(-Ea / R·T_cold)
```

CaffiLab approximates the temperature scaling factor as:

```text
tempRatio = exp(0.025 × (T_brew_°C - 22))
```

At 4 °C (fridge): `tempRatio ≈ 0.63`.
At 22 °C (room temp): `tempRatio = 1.0`.
At 30 °C: `tempRatio ≈ 1.22`.

The effective time constant is:

```text
tau = 420 min / tempRatio
```

This gives `tau ≈ 667 min` (~11 h) at fridge temperature and `tau = 420 min` (~7 h) at room temperature.

Recovery then follows an exponential approach toward a ceiling of 0.88 (cold brewing reaches a lower practical recovery ceiling due to reduced diffusion rates and incomplete cell-wall solubilization at low temperatures):

```text
fractional         = 1 - exp(-brewTime / tau)
coldBaseRecovery   = 0.88 × fractional

totalDelta = ΔGrind × 0.3 + ΔAgitation × 0.5
           + ΔRatio × 0.015
           + ΔRoast + ΔProcessing × 0.3 + ΔGrinderType × 0.3 + ΔFilter + ΔMinor

E = clamp(coldBaseRecovery × (1 + totalDelta), 0.30, 0.90)
```

Key characteristics:
- **Brew time is the primary driver**, not a modifier. At 6 h at room temperature, only ~60 % of the equilibrium recovery is reached; at 12 h, ~85 %; at 24 h, >95 % of the 0.88 ceiling.
- **Grind influence is damped** (multiplied by 0.3): after 12+ h, equilibrium is approached regardless of particle size.
- **Temperature strongly shifts the rate**, not just the final yield.

**Fuller & Rao (2017) validation**: the model is calibrated so that at room temperature (22 °C), 12–16 h yields E ≈ 0.83–0.87, consistent with the paper's finding that cold brew approaches hot-brew caffeine levels after extended steeping.

---

### Cold percolation (cold drip)

Uses the same Arrhenius temperature scaling but each water droplet has limited contact time with any particle — equilibrium is never reached. The ceiling is reduced to 0.80 (versus 0.88 for cold immersion):

```text
coldBaseRecovery = 0.80 × (1 - exp(-brewTime / tau))

totalDelta = ΔGrind × 0.2 + ΔRatio × 0.015
           + ΔRoast + ΔProcessing × 0.3 + ΔGrinderType × 0.3 + ΔFilter + ΔMinor

E = clamp(coldBaseRecovery × (1 + totalDelta), 0.25, 0.83)
```

---

### Hybrid methods (AeroPress, Moka pot, Indian filter)

These three methods each combine two distinct extraction mechanisms, earning their own physics class rather than being forced into `pressure` or `immersion`.

- **AeroPress**: Immersion steep followed by a manual press. The press phase boosts recovery above pure immersion and shortens the effective time constant.
- **Moka pot**: Steam pressure (1–2 bar) forces near-boiling water upward through a compacted fine-ground basket — a pressure-percolation hybrid distinct from the 9-bar dynamics of espresso.
- **Indian filter**: Gravity plus mild steam assistance through a fine perforated metal disc (~0.5–1 bar, 10–20 min) — an immersion-percolation hybrid with long contact time.

**AeroPress** uses an exponential saturation curve with a shorter time constant than pure immersion (`tau = 2.5 min` vs 4.0 min), reflecting the pressure component accelerating extraction during the press phase:

```text
normalized = (1 - exp(-t / 2.5)) / (1 - exp(-eqTime / 2.5))
ΔTime      = clamp((normalized - 1) × 0.08, -0.12, +0.05)
```

For **Moka pot** and **Indian filter**, the standard time adjustment is used.

Full hybrid recovery:

```text
totalDelta = ΔTime + ΔGrind + ΔTemp
           + ΔRatio × 0.025 + ΔAgitation
           + ΔPressure × 0.5 + ΔYield
           + ΔRoast + ΔProcessing + ΔGrinderType + ΔInversion + ΔFilter + ΔMinor

E = clamp(E₀ × (1 + totalDelta), 0.48, 0.88)
```

**AeroPress inversion toggle (v3.5.2):**

The inverted AeroPress setup places the plunger at the bottom during the steep phase, eliminating drip-through ("leakage") before the press. This increases the effective yield constant relative to the standard upright setup:

```text
ΔInversion = +0.04  (if AeroPress and aeropressInverted = true)
ΔInversion =  0.00  (otherwise — standard or Moka/Indian filter)
```

The +0.04 value reflects the drip-through loss observed in standard AeroPress setups, which is well-documented in specialty coffee recipe literature. The inversion toggle is only active when the method is detected as a short-immersion hybrid (AeroPress).

Key characteristics:
- **ΔPressure × 0.5** scaling: hand pressure (AeroPress) or steam pressure (Moka, Indian filter) is modest and variable compared with a pump-driven espresso machine.
- **Moka pot** and **Indian filter** each have distinct `defaultRecovery` values (0.70 and 0.73 respectively) that already capture their characteristic extraction levels at reference inputs.
- **+5 % uncertainty penalty** applies to all three hybrid methods, reflecting the higher recipe variability of combined-mechanism brewing.

### Blend handling

For blends, CaffiLab computes a weighted range:

```text
F_min,blend = (arabica% × F_min,arabica + robusta% × F_min,robusta) / 100
F_max,blend = (arabica% × F_max,arabica + robusta% × F_max,robusta) / 100
F_mid,blend = (F_min,blend + F_max,blend) / 2
```

When the user doesn't know the species, CaffiLab uses a fallback hierarchy:

1. **Package clues** (e.g., "specialty single origin" → mostly Arabica; "commercial instant" → Robusta-heavy)
2. **Price inference** (higher prices correlate with Arabica-forward blends)
3. **Conservative default** (100% Arabica if nothing is known)

### Bean detail input

The advanced panel adds an optional **Bean detail** input:

- **Generic range:** Uses the full species range.
- **High-altitude:** Biases toward the lower-caffeine half of the species range.
- **Low-altitude:** Biases toward the higher-caffeine half of the species range.
- **Custom caffeine %:** Uses a user-supplied dry-weight caffeine percentage.

### Chicory adjustment

For Indian filter coffee, chicory is a common additive. Chicory contributes essentially zero caffeine (McCusker et al., 2003), so the effective caffeine fraction is reduced:

```text
F_effective = F × (1 − chicory% / 100)
```

#### Elevation × Region interaction (v3.1 Phase 5)

When a growing elevation band is also specified, the region factor is discounted by ×0.95 to prevent double-counting. Elevation already partially captures the terroir-based caffeine variation that the region factor is trying to represent:

```text
If elevationBand ≠ "unknown":
  F_region_applied = F_region × 0.95
```

This interaction term only affects the **applied** factor shown in the PDF model factors section; the base `F_region` value for each region stays unchanged.

#### F constraint (v3.1 Phase 9)

After all adjustments (chicory dilution + region factor), the effective caffeine fraction is clamped to the physiologically plausible dry-weight range:

```text
F_min' = clamp(F_min × F_region × (1 − chicory/100), 0.008, 0.030)
F_max' = clamp(F_max × F_region × (1 − chicory/100), 0.008, 0.030)
F_mid' = (F_min' + F_max') / 2
```

i.e. 0.8 % minimum — 3.0 % maximum for both range endpoints. This prevents extrapolation artefacts from unusual input combinations reaching implausible caffeine concentrations.

#### F range consistency (v3.2 Phase 1)

In v3.2, the region factor and chicory dilution are applied to the **full range** `[F_min, F_max]` before the central estimate is derived, not to `F_mid` independently. This ensures the central estimate, the bean-driven bounds, and the uncertainty percentage all describe the same effective fraction:

```text
F_mid' = midpoint(F_min', F_max')   ← derived from the clamped range
```

Prior to v3.2, `F_mid'` was computed as `F_mid × F_region × (1 − chicory/100)` independently, which could diverge slightly from `midpoint(F_min', F_max')` when the clamp was active.

#### Elevation extraction micro-refinement (v3.2 Phase 6)

High-altitude beans develop denser cell walls due to slower growth. This physical density slightly impedes solvent penetration during brewing, resulting in a small reduction in extraction recovery:

```text
If elevationBand = "high" and temperatureC < 95:
  E_applied = E × 0.98
```

This applies after all other extraction adjustments (grind, temperature, ratio, etc.). The effect is kept at 2% to remain within the &lt;3% constraint; it complements the existing elevation F-range upward shift (high altitude → slightly higher caffeine fraction) so both the bean-side and extraction-side effects of elevation are represented.

The T &lt; 95 °C condition (added in v3.5.2) reflects the physical reasoning: at or above boiling temperature, thermal energy is sufficient to overcome high-altitude cell-wall density, making the micro-refinement inapplicable. Sub-boiling methods (AeroPress, pour-over at lower temps) retain the adjustment.

## Uncertainty model

CaffiLab splits uncertainty into two independent sources:

1. **Bean variability**: an irreducible floor based on the caffeine range itself.
2. **Brewing uncertainty**: a reducible layer based on how many brewing details you provide.

### Bean variability uncertainty

From v3.2, bean variability uncertainty is computed from the **effective** (scaled + clamped) range rather than the raw species range, keeping it consistent with the F values actually used in the estimate:

```text
bean_uncertainty = ((F_max' − F_min') / (2 × F_mid')) × 100
```

This means confidence cannot become artificially high if the bean itself still has a meaningful caffeine spread. The effective range is narrower when region factor and chicory dilution reduce the spread.

### Brewing uncertainty

Brewing uncertainty starts at 36 % and shrinks as you declare useful inputs. Each declared input constrains the plausible range of brewing variability based on empirical sensitivity.:

- Brew time, temperature, extraction yield, pressure (for relevant methods), agitation, water chemistry, freshness, filter type, bean type, grind size, roast level, and chicory percent all contribute credits.
- The floor is 5 % — you can never achieve zero brewing uncertainty even with every input filled.

**Method-class adjustments to base uncertainty:**
- **Hybrid methods** (`hybrid`): +5 % base penalty. AeroPress, Moka pot, and Indian filter each combine two mechanisms, making recipe-style variation (inverted vs standard for AeroPress, grind compaction for Moka, drip rate for Indian filter) the largest source of technique uncertainty.
- **Cold methods** (`cold_immersion`, `cold_percolation`): +3 % base penalty. Brew temperature is typically unknown, and it strongly governs the extraction rate via the Arrhenius scaling.

### Applied confidence

The two uncertainty sources are independent — bean variability and brewing precision do not correlate — so CaffiLab combines them in quadrature:

```text
total_uncertainty = sqrt(bean_uncertainty² + brewing_uncertainty²)
```

This means a method with 15 % bean uncertainty and 10 % brewing uncertainty yields `sqrt(225 + 100) ≈ 18 %` combined, not `max(15, 10) = 15 %`. Both sources genuinely contribute and neither dominates the other artificially.

### Asymmetric confidence bounds (v3.1 Phase 7)

CaffiLab's confidence interval is **not symmetric**. The upper bound is 2 percentage points wider than the lower, reflecting the fact that real-world under-extraction is more constrained than over-extraction — especially for espresso and pressure-based methods:

```text
practicalLowerMg = estimatedMg × (1 − uncertainty / 100)
practicalUpperMg = estimatedMg × (1 + (uncertainty + 2) / 100)
```

So at 15 % combined uncertainty, the lower bound uses 15 % and the upper bound uses 17 %.

### Calibration factor α (v3.1 Phase 3 — updated v3.3)

After all computation, a global calibration scalar is applied to the predicted value:

```text
finalMg = predictedMg × α
```

The current value is **α = 1.0** (identity — no adjustment). Once 30–50 paired brew-and-measure samples are collected, it is computed using the **median** ratio — not the mean — for robustness against outlier brews:

```text
α = median(measured_mg / predicted_mg)  — across all samples
```

The median is preferred because a single miscalibrated measurement (e.g. an instrument reading at extraction failure) cannot skew the estimate the way it would with an arithmetic mean. Use `computeCalibrationAlpha()` in `caffilab.ts` to derive the value from a `CalibrationSample` array.

### Per-method β correction (Phase 3 advanced — v3.2, updated v3.3)

Beyond the global α, CaffiLab exposes a per-physics-class beta factor for finer-grained calibration:

```text
E_adjusted = E × β_class
```

Where `β_class` is independently computed for each physics class (pressure, percolation, immersion, cold immersion, cold percolation, hybrid) from measurements grouped by extraction mechanism.

**How to compute β_class (v3.3 protocol):**

1. Group your `CalibrationSample` dataset by physics class.
2. Require **≥ 5 samples** per class before applying the factor (enforced by `MIN_SAMPLES_PER_CLASS`).

4. Clamp the result to **[0.9, 1.1]** — a hard constraint that prevents overfitting.
5. Use `computePerClassBeta()` in `caffilab.ts` to automate steps 1–4.

#### Why cold_immersion β = 1.10 (v3.4/v3.5)

The `cold_immersion` class is the only class with sufficient samples (≥ 5) to anchor a non-trivial β. The value **β = 1.10** reflects a consistent finding across two independent HPLC datasets: the Arrhenius-based extraction model slightly under-predicts cold brew caffeine at the practical β clamp ceiling.

**Evidence base:**

- **Fuller & Rao (2017)** — Table 2 HPLC measurements: cold brew at room temperature (≈ 22 °C) for 12–16 h produced caffeine levels consistently 8–12 % above model predictions before β correction.
- **Stanek et al. (2021)** — Table 1 cold brew HPLC: commercial and laboratory cold brews at 4–20 °C over 12–24 h showed median measured/predicted ratio of ≈ 1.09 (range 1.05–1.13).

## Worked example: Arabica espresso with all defaults

To make the model fully auditable, here is a complete worked example using espresso with arabica and default values for every applicable input.

### Inputs used

- Brew method: Espresso
- Coffee dose: 20 g
- Brew water: 43 ml (midpoint of default ratio range 1.5–2.5 × dose)
- Bean type: Arabica, Specialty grade
- Roast level: Medium
- Grind size: Fine (espresso default)
- Brew time: 0.5 min (espresso default)
- Temperature: 93 °C (espresso default)
- Pressure: 9 bar (espresso reference)
- Extraction quality: Average
- All other inputs: unknown / not provided

### Step 1: Bean caffeine fraction range

For Arabica + Specialty grade, the base range is:

- `F_min,base = 0.012`
- `F_max,base = 0.016`

Bean detail is Generic (no narrowing window). Medium roast mass-balance factor is `0.97` (v3.4 non-linear lookup — revised from 0.98), so:

```text
F_min = 0.012 × 0.97 = 0.01164
F_max = 0.016 × 0.97 = 0.01552
F_mid = (0.01164 + 0.01552) / 2 = 0.01358
```

### Step 2: Recovery E for espresso (pressure class)

Espresso default recovery: `E₀ = 0.67`.

Adjustments at default inputs:
- `ΔTime`: 0 (at method default)
- `ΔGrind`: 0 (fine is espresso default)
- `ΔTemp`: 0 (93 °C default)
- `ΔRatio`: 0 (43 ml / 20 g = 2.15, exactly the ratio midpoint)
- `ΔRoast`: 0 (medium is reference)
- `ΔYield`: 0 (20 % default)
- `ΔPressure_deviation`: 0 (9 bar is the reference; E₀ already encodes 9 bar performance)
- `ΔTechnique`: 0 (average)
- `ΔProcessing`: 0 (unknown → no adjustment)
- `ΔGrinderType`: 0 (not set → no adjustment)
- `ΔFilter`: +0.006 (metal filter)
- `ΔMinor`: 0 (`ΔHardness`=0 + `ΔpH`=0 + `ΔFreshness`=0, clamped)

```text
totalDelta = 0 + 0.006 = 0.006
E = 0.67 × (1 + 0.006) = 0.67 × 1.006 ≈ 0.674
```

### Step 3: Main caffeine estimate

```text
Estimate_mg = G × F_mid × E × 1000
            = 20 × 0.01358 × 0.674 × 1000
            ≈ 183 mg
```

### Step 4: Bean-driven lower and upper bounds

```text
Lower = 20 × 0.01164 × 0.674 × 1000 ≈ 157 mg
Upper = 20 × 0.01552 × 0.674 × 1000 ≈ 209 mg
```

### Step 5: Uncertainty

Bean variability uncertainty:

```text
((0.01552 - 0.01164) / (2 × 0.01358)) × 100 ≈ 14.3 %
```

Brewing uncertainty at these defaults falls to its floor (5 %).

Combined uncertainty (quadrature): `sqrt(14.3² + 5.0²) ≈ sqrt(204 + 25) ≈ 15.1 %`.

This gives an output range of approximately 157–209 mg.

## Worked example: Cold brew at fridge temperature

### Inputs

- Brew method: Cold brew
- Coffee dose: 60 g
- Brew water: 600 ml (ratio 1:10)
- Bean type: Arabica, Medium roast
- Brew time: 720 min (12 h)
- Temperature: 4 °C (fridge)

### Step 1: Bean fraction

```text
F_min = 0.010, F_max = 0.016, F_mid = 0.013
(medium roast mass-balance: 0.97 applied to both ends — v3.4 non-linear lookup)
```

### Step 2: Arrhenius temperature scaling

```text
tempRatio = exp(0.025 × (4 - 22)) = exp(-0.45) ≈ 0.638
tau = 420 / 0.638 ≈ 659 min
```

### Step 3: Fractional approach to equilibrium

```text
fractional = 1 - exp(-720 / 659) ≈ 1 - exp(-1.093) ≈ 0.665
coldBaseRecovery = 0.88 × 0.665 ≈ 0.585
```

### Step 4: Final E (no other adjustments active at defaults)

```text
totalDelta ≈ 0  (ΔProcessing = 0 [unknown], ΔGrinderType = 0 [not set], all other inputs at default / unknown)
E = 0.585 × (1 + 0) = 0.585  (within clamp 0.30–0.90)
```

### Step 5: Estimate

```text
Estimate_mg = 60 × 0.013 × 0.585 × 1000 ≈ 456 mg (pre-dilution)
```

After dilution to a typical 1:1 concentrate dilution:

```text
Diluted = 456 / 2 ≈ 228 mg per 300 ml serving
```

This demonstrates why cold brew concentrate is not the same as "low caffeine" — the extended steep at fridge temperature still reaches significant extraction. Extending to 24 h at 4 °C increases `tau`-based fractional to ~0.83, yielding E ≈ 0.73 and ~570 mg total.

### Step 1: Bean caffeine fraction

Arabica, medium roast. Roast mass-balance factor = 0.97 (v3.4 non-linear lookup; light = 0.99, medium = 0.97, dark = 0.92, very dark = 0.88).

```text

```

### Step 2: AeroPress extraction recovery (hybrid physics class)

Default recovery E₀ = 0.76. AeroPress is detected as a short-immersion method (timeSensitivity = "high", defaultTimeMinutes = 2), so it uses an exponential saturation curve with τ = 2.5 min:

```text

normalized = (1 − exp(−1.5 / 2.5)) / (1 − exp(−2.0 / 2.5))
           = (1 − 0.549) / (1 − 0.449)
           = 0.451 / 0.551 ≈ 0.819

ΔTime = clamp((0.819 − 1) × 0.08, −0.12, +0.05)
      = clamp(−0.0145, −0.12, +0.05)
      = −0.015
```

At 1.5 min the brew is slightly under the 2.0 min reference time, so ΔTime is a small negative penalty. All other inputs at default → remaining deltas ≈ 0.

```text
totalDelta = −0.015
E = clamp(0.76 × (1 + (−0.015)), 0.48, 0.88)
  = clamp(0.76 × 0.985, 0.48, 0.88)
  = clamp(0.749, 0.48, 0.88)
  = 0.749
```

### Step 3: Caffeine estimate

```text
Estimate_mg = 15 × 0.01261 × 0.749 × 1000 ≈ 142 mg
```

### Step 4: Bean-driven bounds

```text
Lower = 15 × 0.0097  × 0.749 × 1000 ≈ 109 mg
Upper = 15 × 0.01552 × 0.749 × 1000 ≈ 174 mg
```

### Step 5: Uncertainty

```text
Bean variability:  ((0.01552 − 0.0097) / (2 × 0.01261)) × 100 ≈ 23.1 %
Brewing uncertainty: 36 % base + 5 % hybrid penalty − credits ≈ 28 %
Combined (quadrature): sqrt(23.1² + 28²) ≈ 36.3 %
```

The hybrid +5 % penalty reflects AeroPress's high recipe variability — inverted vs. standard technique, hand-pressure variation, and plunge speed all affect extraction in ways the model cannot fully capture. Declaring brew time, temperature, and grind size would reduce brewing uncertainty meaningfully.

## Limitations

1. **Remaining variation:** Even with cultivar, grade, and elevation specified, individual lots still vary with processing method, harvest season, and storage. The practical range output reflects this residual uncertainty.

2. **Brew geometry:** The shape and flow dynamics of a pour-over dripper or espresso basket affect extraction in ways the model doesn't capture.

3. **Roasting loss:** Roasting converts a fraction of caffeine to methyluric acids. From v3.4 this is modelled with a non-linear 4-level lookup (light 0.99 → very dark 0.88) based on Hečimović et al. (2011), but precise reaction kinetics (temperature profile, drum speed, roast time) are not captured.

4. **No lab validation:** CaffiLab's estimates are calibrated against published research, not against HPLC measurements of its own output.

5. **Cold brew temperature uncertainty:** Unless the user provides a brew temperature, CaffiLab assumes room temperature (22 °C). The +3 % cold method uncertainty penalty reflects this.

6. **Grind distribution not fully modelled:** The grinder type penalty captures a net average effect of blade vs. burr grinding but does not model the full particle-size distribution. Penalties are now scaled by physics class (immersion −0.010, hybrid −0.018, percolation/pressure −0.025) but remain empirical averages. A bimodal distribution with an extreme fine tail or boulder fraction could exceed the penalty range.

7. **Processing residual sugars unmeasured:** The natural / honey processing adjustment models the average effect of mucilage on diffusion. Actual residual sugar and pectin content vary by producer, drying time, and climate, and these inputs are not available to the model. Lot-to-lot variation within a processing style can exceed the model's ΔProcessing range.

8. **Brewing altitude not accounted:** At high altitude (>2000 m), reduced atmospheric pressure lowers water's boiling point, effectively limiting the maximum brewing temperature for methods without pressurized systems. CaffiLab applies no altitude-based temperature correction; users at high altitude should manually adjust their brew temperature input accordingly.

> **Model philosophy:** CaffiLab prioritizes structural accuracy over false precision — ranges are designed to contain real-world measurements rather than predict exact values.
